Tribal Leadership

Leveraging Natural Groups to Build a Thriving Organization

The same map of consciousness, drawn for groups: from ‘life sucks’ through ‘I am great’ to ‘we are great’ — and past it. Written about companies, works on families, friendships, any tribe you are in.

Dave Logan, John King, and Halee Fischer-Wright are management consultants who studied roughly 24,000 people across dozens of organizations. Published in 2008, the book maps human groups — companies, but really any tribe — onto five stages of culture, from open hostility to a rare stage where competition stops mattering entirely.

A few lines worth keeping:

Stage two. People talk as if the organization’s concerns have nothing to do with them, as if they don’t care what happens. They do only the bare minimum required, showing zero initiative or interest — while performing enthusiasm for leadership. The undertone of their communication: no effort will change anything, so the only sane move is not to try.

Stage three. People throw themselves into everything around them, energetically and with real interest. But listen closely and they mostly talk about themselves — trying to look smarter and better than everyone else. They believe they’re solving the team’s problems; their behavior shows it’s personal interest driving them.

Stage four. Teams are focused on shared values and shared goals. Information moves freely between members. Relationships are built on values, not utility. People here habitually ask: “What’s the next right move?” — and form partnerships to do whatever matters right now. “We” leads their speech, not “I.”

Stage five. Your tribe rarely talks about competitors, if at all — except to sharpen the contrast with its own culture. The dominant subject is the boundless possibility ahead, limited only by imagination and the group’s own commitment. There is almost no fear, stress, or workplace conflict. People talk as if the whole world is watching — and often it is, because they’re producing historic results.

People who’ve had the realization read books differently — underlining passages for other people, hoping to plant something true and change their lives.
