How I Found Freedom in an Unfree World

A Handbook for Personal Liberty

Most of the chains we live in are ones we accepted — moral obligations, guilt, the duty to control others. Freedom isn’t won by changing the world; it’s found by quietly stepping out of the traps and living the life you actually want, starting now.

Harry Browne, an investment advisor and later a Libertarian Party presidential candidate, published this handbook in 1973. He argues that unfreedom is mostly self-imposed — a set of “traps” like guilt, unselfishness, and the belief you owe your happiness to others — and that escaping them takes no political change, only the decision to stop accepting false obligations and act on direct alternatives you actually control.

A few lines worth keeping:

Life is to be lived, not sacrificed.

A free person has no one to blame.

You can only decide for yourself.

You’re not as indispensable to others as you may have believed.

And so one day you may discover that you’ve achieved a new freedom, an emotional freedom that’s greater than any you’ve known before — freedom from the urge to control others.

Freedom is the opportunity to live your life as you want to live it.

Your life is all you have. What could possibly be important enough to warrant throwing that life away?

The traps are paper chains. And the individual who sees them for what they are will know that it was his choice to be enslaved; he hasn’t been overpowered.

Everyone is selfish. Selfishness isn’t really an issue, because everyone selfishly seeks his own happiness.

The gigantic myth called “society” that rules so many lives doesn’t even exist.

I don’t think you can really be free until you’re willing to let others be free.
