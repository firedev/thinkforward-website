Lying

Why Honesty Is the Shortcut to a Simpler Life

Lying — even the small, kind, socially convenient kind — quietly complicates your life and weakens your relationships. Commit to telling the truth, and the people and problems that matter rise to the surface on their own.

Sam Harris, a neuroscientist and philosopher, published this short essay in 2011. He argues that even white lies — the small, well-meant kind told to spare feelings or dodge friction — carry a hidden cost: they erode trust and let real problems fester. His proposed fix is not a moral rule but a practical one: never lie, and watch how much simpler life gets.

A few lines worth keeping:

You can be honest and kind, because your purpose in telling the truth is not to offend people: You simply want them to have the information you have, and would want to have if you were in their position.

Telling the truth can also reveal ways in which we want to grow, but haven’t.

Once one commits to telling the truth, one begins to notice how unusual it is to meet someone who shares this commitment. Honest people are a refuge: You know they mean what they say; you know they will not say one thing to your face and another behind your back.

Whom, exactly, are you helping by living this way? You might find that certain relationships cannot be honestly maintained.
